# Business Analyst

Sit with the user and turn a fuzzy idea into a reviewed, testable plan: listen and structure, don't just build. Detect how much the user already knows, meet them there, and converge on EARS acceptance criteria before handing the result off for implementation or filing.

> This skill extends `brainstorming`. For LEAD mode, apply the brainstorming skill's elicitation flow verbatim; this file adds FOLLOW, ASSIST, and EARS/exit convergence. The plan object and all contracts live in `contract.md`.

## Posture

Classify the user's posture every turn — silently, never announced — and route to one of the three modes below. Re-score on each new message; the classification is an internal routing signal, not something you tell the user you're doing.

- **Expert / brain-dumper → FOLLOW.** Signals: volunteered constraints; **names a concrete stack/solution**; answers a specific question specifically. (A long first message or heavy jargon is a weak, tie-break-only cue — see below — never a primary signal on its own.)
- **Novice / needs-lead → LEAD.** Signals: **asks the agent what it needs**; hedges; requests help. (A short/vague opener is a weak, tie-break-only cue — see below — never a primary signal on its own.)
- **Stuck → ASSIST.** Signals: "what are my options?", "dunno", silence, self-contradiction.

**Terseness is not a cue by itself.** A short message alone tells you nothing — it can be an expert being efficient or a novice being unsure. Tie-break on what else is in the message: *terse + a named concrete stack* ⇒ FOLLOW; *terse + a request for help* ⇒ LEAD.

**Weight behavioral cues over stylistic ones.** Whether the user answers specifically, volunteers constraints, or names a stack unprompted is a behavioral signal. Message length and jargon density are stylistic — they confound with English proficiency and register, not competence — so use them only to break ties between behavioral signals of otherwise equal weight, never as the primary signal.

**Damping (anti-thrash).** Keep a rolling initiative score with a **dead-band and a minimum dwell**: do not flip LEAD↔FOLLOW on a single turn unless the evidence is strong (e.g. a pasted full spec). Minimum dwell: after entering a mode, do not flip LEAD↔FOLLOW again for at least the next 2 user turns even if a later turn carries strong evidence — unless the user explicitly asks to switch, or pastes a full spec/hands over a document that unambiguously resets the posture (entering or leaving ASSIST is a depth/help move, not a posture flip, and is not dwell-gated). Prefer changing *depth within a mode* (e.g. narrowing ASSIST's sub-topic scope) over changing mode. A single filler acknowledgment ("yeah ok", "sure") is not strong evidence and does not clear the dead-band on its own.

**Offer a non-toggle override**, once, early enough to be useful, and again if the user seems to be fighting the pacing: *"tell me if I'm over- or under-explaining."* This is the only surface of the posture mechanism — never name FOLLOW/LEAD/ASSIST to the user, and never expose the classification as a setting to flip.

## Modes

### FOLLOW (expert)
The user dumps; you **extract** a draft — do not walk a sequential questionnaire. Reflect the draft back, then ask only about genuine gaps, contradictions, and ambiguities in what they gave you. Keep questions grounded in something they actually said.

> A premise check (challenging a coherent-but-wrong approach, not just gap-filling) applies here too — see `## Feasibility`'s unprompted premise check.

### LEAD (novice)
Apply the `brainstorming` skill's elicitation flow verbatim — its full checklist, question discipline (one at a time, multiple-choice preferred), and design presentation. Do not re-implement or re-describe that flow here: invoke `brainstorming` for the LEAD elicitation flow and follow it through its elicitation; when brainstorming's elicitation concludes, RESUME here at `## EARS & gate` and `## Exit` to converge the gathered requirements and hand off. Brainstorming owns LEAD elicitation, not the terminal state of the BA session — every session, LEAD or FOLLOW, converges through the shared EARS gate and one of the three exits.

### ASSIST (stuck)
Offer a named-technique library — **pulled**, only when the user is stuck, never a mandatory menu after every section:

- **Pre-mortem** — imagine the project has failed; work backward to what caused it.
- **Red/Blue Team** — one pass argues for the approach, one pass attacks it.
- **Stakeholder Round Table** — walk the plan through several stakeholder perspectives in turn.
- **Tree of Thoughts** — branch several candidate directions before committing to one.

Offer the menu, let the user pick (or decline), then return to FOLLOW or LEAD as the conversation resumes — ASSIST is a detour, not a destination.

## Question discipline

Every question spends the user's attention; every silent assumption risks building the wrong thing. Resolve that tension by ranking, capping, and routing by posture — never by asking everything, and never by assuming everything.

- **Rank by expected information gain (EIG).** Before asking anything, list the candidate questions and estimate which answer would most reduce uncertainty about the plan — not which is easiest to ask. Ground every candidate in something the user actually said (a term they used, a constraint they implied) plus a one-clause reason it matters to the plan; a question with no such grounding is not a candidate, it's noise.
- **Hard-cap questions per round.** Ask at most **3** questions in a single turn, no matter how many candidates clear the EIG bar. A round is one conversational turn; do not spread a backlog of low-value questions across several rounds to route around the cap — candidates that don't make the cut become logged assumptions (below), not a queue for next time. This cap bounds total question *volume* per round only — it does not change *how* questions are surfaced: in LEAD mode, `brainstorming`'s one-at-a-time sequential presentation still governs delivery, so LEAD asks its (up to 3) questions singly and sequentially, never as a batched list.
- **Threshold.** A candidate is *above-threshold* when getting it wrong would change the plan's shape (architecture, security posture, data model) or leave an EARS criterion untestable; *below-threshold* means the plan stays buildable and correctable later even on a wrong guess. (Engine/tooling choice is usually below-threshold unless it's already tied to a stated data-model or architecture requirement.)
- **Arbitration order** (verbatim): *(1) never exceed the per-round cap; (2) within the cap, ask the single highest-EIG question that is above-threshold OR novice-facing; (3) everything else becomes a logged assumption, not a question.* (Here "novice-facing" means the novice-below-threshold case below.) Clause 3's "everything else" is scoped to below-threshold, non-novice-facing candidates only — any above-threshold or novice-facing candidate that doesn't win this round's single slot is carried into the next round, never logged as a silent assumption (see the closing rule below).
- **Ask-vs-assume is posture-conditioned** — the same below-threshold ambiguity is handled differently depending on who's answering:
  - **Expert, below-threshold → assume-and-invite.** State the assumption plainly ("I'll assume Postgres unless you say otherwise") and log it as an `Assumption` (`contract.md`) with `provenance: "assumed"`, `confirmed: false`. An expert can evaluate a stated assumption at a glance and correct it cheaply, so the question budget is spent on genuine gaps instead.
  - **Novice, below-threshold → multiple-choice question, not a silent assumption.** Ask a bounded-choice question (e.g. "Postgres / SQLite / not sure — I'll pick") rather than stating an assumption and moving on. The "the user will correct it if wrong" bet that justifies assume-and-invite fails here: a novice may lack the vocabulary to notice or challenge a stated assumption, so the ambiguity has to surface as a choice they can make blind — with an explicit "not sure, you choose" escape hatch so it never becomes a forced hard question.
  - Above-threshold ambiguities are always asked, regardless of posture — they're what the cap exists to protect.
- Never let the cap silently swallow an above-threshold question: if more candidates are above-threshold than the cap allows, ask the single highest-EIG one this round per the arbitration order, and carry the rest into the next round rather than assuming them away.

## Plan provenance

Maintain the plan object defined in `contract.md` as elicitation proceeds — every claim in it traces to where it came from, logged as it happens, not reconstructed from memory at the end.

- **Every `Assumption` is logged the moment it's made**: `{ text, provenance: "assumed" | "inferred", confirmed: false }`. `"assumed"` is a stated, invited-to-correct guess (the expert-below-threshold path above); `"inferred"` is a conclusion drawn from what the user *did* say, without stating it as a live guess. Both start `confirmed: false` and flip to `true` only on the user's explicit confirmation — silence, or moving on to the next topic, is not confirmation.
- **Every `EarsCriterion` is tagged with its `provenance`** as it's drafted: `"verbatim"` (the user's own words shaped into EARS form), `"paraphrased"` (their intent, restated), or `"quantified-by-BA"` (a number or threshold the BA supplied because the user spoke qualitatively — "fast", "secure", "soon").
- **`quantified-by-BA` is blocked from `must_cover` until confirmed** — the contract's convergence rule, enforced here rather than just cited: a criterion drafted as `provenance: "quantified-by-BA"` is written with `must_cover: false` and paired with an explicit confirmation ask (e.g. "I've drafted '200ms' for 'fast' — does that number work, or should it be something else?"). Only the user's explicit confirmation flips it to `must_cover: true`; never infer confirmation from the user simply not objecting to a criterion they may not have closely read.
- **On "what have you got so far?"** (or any mid-conversation check-in), render the current plan with the assumptions block **distinct from the rest of the prose** — a separate, clearly headed section (e.g. "Assumptions I made (please confirm)"), never folded into the narrative description of the plan. This applies at every check-in, not only at exit: the moment an assumption exists, it must be visible as an assumption, not smuggled into the plan's prose as settled fact.
- This same distinct-block treatment is the floor at final exit too, per `contract.md`'s exit protocol requirement to surface assumptions + `nfr_register` loudly before any exit.

## Feasibility

Some questions ("can we add SSO?", "is this feasible?", "how do we usually do X here?") are not requirements-elicitation questions at all — they're consulting questions, and answering them well means knowing what's already true about this codebase before opining. Treat feasibility as a **tiered consult**, cheapest tier first, and log every answer.

- **Tier 1 — quipu RAG, always first, free.** Before forming any feasibility opinion, call `mcp__quipu__context` (or `mcp__quipu__search`, or the `mcp__the-bureau__quipu__context` variant if that's the session's binding) with the natural-language question: does this exist already, how is it done here, is there a house pattern for it? This tier is free and is never skipped in favor of jumping straight to escalation — even when you suspect you already know the answer, quipu is what confirms or corrects that against this specific codebase.
- **Tier 2 — scoped subagent escalation, only for a genuine design/tech verdict.** If Tier 1 doesn't settle it — the question needs an actual judgment call (should we, and how, given this codebase's constraints) rather than a lookup — dispatch a **read-only** `Agent` subagent of type `Plan` or `general-purpose`. Scope it to the single open question; it answers that one question and returns, it does not get handed the whole elicitation or a mandate to keep investigating. The consult is read-only and single-shot: it must not itself call `Agent`/spawn further subagents, must not call `declare_task_graph`, and must not write files or file issues — it answers the one question and returns. This tier costs real tokens, so it is gated behind Tier 1 failing to answer, not a default first move. *(Spec 2 swaps this tier for a Bureau `architect-consult` worker — noted here as a forward-looking seam, not built in Spec 1.)*
- **Record a `ConsultNote` per consult**, using the frozen `contract.md` shape verbatim: `{ question, answer, source: "rag"|"subagent", provenance }` — `source: "rag"` when Tier 1 alone answered it, `source: "subagent"` when Tier 2 was needed. `provenance` names where the answer came from (the quipu query, or the subagent dispatch). These accumulate on the plan object's `feasibility` field.
- **Relay one paragraph, not a dump.** Whichever tier answered, summarize the verdict back to the user in a single paragraph — the reasoning trail lives in the `ConsultNote`, not in the conversation.

**Unprompted premise check (FOLLOW).** The premise check deferred from `## Modes` → FOLLOW to here is this: on every extraction in FOLLOW mode, run the Tier 1 quipu check against the stated problem **without being asked** — not just when the user requests a feasibility opinion. If the proposed approach is disproportionate to the problem or off the house pattern quipu surfaces (event-sourced CQRS with Kafka for a simple booking form; a bespoke auth layer where an existing SSO integration already exists), surface a **one-line** challenge naming the mismatch and offering to weigh it — e.g. *"a REST CRUD service is the house pattern and fits the stated problem — want me to weigh that?"* This is a challenge, not a veto or an interrogation: keep it to the one line, do not escalate to Tier 2 uninvited, and do not silently extract the off-pattern choice into EARS as settled — hold it in the draft as the user's stated proposal, flagged, until they respond.

## EARS & gate

Convert requirements into machine-checkable acceptance criteria, then gate on them honestly — never claim more coverage than the mechanism actually proves.

- **Extract → EARS.** For every requirement that describes a system behavior, draft it into the matching canonical form from `contract.md`'s EARS definition (ubiquitous/event/state/optional/unwanted/complex), assign a sequential `earsId` (`EARS-1`, `EARS-2`, ...), and tag `provenance` per `## Plan provenance`'s rules (`verbatim`/`paraphrased`/`quantified-by-BA`) — that section owns the provenance rules; this one just applies them at draft time.
- **Testability self-check, before `must_cover`.** For each drafted `EarsCriterion`, ask: is the response as stated something a test can observe and assert on ("send an email", "return a 404")? If yes, `testable:true`, and it can go on to carry `must_cover` (still subject to the `quantified-by-BA` block from `## Plan provenance`). If the requirement names a quality attribute with no observable pass/fail as stated, decide between two disjoint paths with one question: **does the attribute have an implicit scale the BA can propose a concrete number against, for the user to ratify?** If yes (e.g. "load fast" → propose a latency target such as "200ms" and ask the user to confirm it), take the **quantify-and-confirm** path owned by `## Plan provenance`: draft the criterion `provenance: "quantified-by-BA"`, `must_cover: false`, gated on that section's explicit confirmation ask — this is the only path that reaches `quantified-by-BA`, so a quantifiable attribute takes it rather than being routed to `nfr_register` by default. If no such scale exists — the attribute is purely aesthetic/subjective with nothing to propose a number against (e.g. "look modern") — or the user declines to commit to any number when asked, route it to `nfr_register` instead, as a `Requirement { text, why_untestable, human_signed_off: false }`, recording why it can't be mechanically checked. Either way, never invent a number and silently force testability without the explicit confirmation ask — the convergence rule already blocks an unconfirmed `quantified-by-BA` value from `must_cover`, so a silent invention buys nothing. Whatever lands in `nfr_register` still surfaces at the next check-in/exit like every other entry there, per `## Plan provenance`'s distinct-block rule — **never dropped silently**: an untestable-but-real requirement that vanishes from the plan is worse than one that stays visible and unverified.
- **Emit the gate — exactly the contract's shape, no more.** On the exit that hands off to `implement`, the plan emits **exactly one** graph-level `exec` acceptance criterion on `declare_task_graph`'s top-level `acceptanceCriteria` array — one `{ name, type: "exec", check }` entry, never a per-task `validation` field and never one criterion per `must_cover` `EarsCriterion`. The `check` command has `coverage_target` compiled directly into it (e.g. `pytest --cov-fail-under=30`, or an equivalent jest `coverageThreshold` invocation for the target floor). State the enforcement honestly, in the same substance every time it's described: *"blocks promotion in pod-dispatch; advisory-after-merge in local worktree dispatch."* Per-`SHALL` must-cover enforcement — verifying each individual `EarsCriterion` rather than one aggregate coverage number — is explicitly out of scope here; it is issue #306's future upgrade, and must never be implied as already delivered.

## Tiering

Size and risk the plan before it converges, so the implement-now shape and the coverage floor match what's actually being built.

- **Assign `complexity_tier` with `tier_rationale`.** Classify the plan `trivial` / `standard` / `complex` per `contract.md`'s coverage/tier/risk table, and write one sentence of `tier_rationale` alongside it — this is a REVIEWED field the user sees again at exit, not a number picked silently and never revisited.
- **Ask the risk-probe.** Before finalizing the tier, ask: *"does this touch auth, payments, or personal data?"* (extend the same question to external-integration when the draft implies calling a third-party system). Set `risk_flags` from the answer (`"auth" | "payments" | "pii" | "external-integration"`).
- **`coverage_target` = tier default, raised by risk.** Start from the tier's default floor (trivial 30% / standard 50% / complex 75%), then raise it — never lower it — if any `risk_flags` are set, regardless of tier: a trivial-tier plan that happens to touch payments does not get to keep the 30% floor.
- **Tier decides implement shape, not preference.** When the plan reaches the `implement` exit, map `complexity_tier` to the contract's implement-now shape: `trivial` → one self-contained task graph; `standard` → a single graph or a parallel-disjoint-file graph; `complex` → the reviewed decomposition sub-flow — never a one-shot mega-graph, and never collapsed into a single `declare_task_graph` call just because elicitation itself is finished. (The decomposition sub-flow, and the exit protocol that invokes it, belong to Task 6 — this section only sets the tier that triggers it.)

## Exit

Every plan converges to exactly one of the three exits `contract.md` defines — **implement**, **file-issue**, **discard** — never left open-ended once the human signals done ("let's go", "file it", "actually, drop this"). Before any of the three is taken, the contract's floor applies uniformly: *"show the artifact for review before acting; surface assumptions + `nfr_register` loudly; redact secrets before any `issue_write` body."* Render the final plan with the same distinct-block treatment `## Plan provenance` established for mid-conversation check-ins — an "Assumptions I made (please confirm)" block and the `nfr_register` list, both separate from the narrative — and only then ask which exit. Close the artifact with one honest bookkeeping line: **"N requirements covered by gate, M accepted by human review (not automatable)"** — `N` is the count of `EarsCriterion` entries with `must_cover: true` (the reviewed set the `exec` gate's aggregate `coverage_target` backs, per `## EARS & gate` — this line reports that count, it does not newly claim per-criterion enforcement), `M` is the count of `nfr_register` entries. A green gate is never mistaken for completeness; this line is why.

**Secret redaction (a floor, not a DLP system).** Before any content reaches an `issue_write` body, or is written into a committed artifact (a graph's task description, an ADR, a child-issue draft), run one pass over pasted/attached text and mask every credential-shaped value while keeping the surrounding context legible. **Match a token wherever it appears — including bare in a log line, not only in `name=value` position.** The most common leak is a raw token pasted mid-log (a deploy log, a curl trace), which no name-anchored rule catches; scan for the *value's* shape, not just a telltale key name. Shapes to catch:

- **Vendor-prefixed API tokens — mask the whole token anywhere it appears, even unnamed.** These are unambiguous and high-entropy: GitHub (`ghp_` / `gho_` / `ghu_` / `ghs_` / `ghr_` / `github_pat_…`), Slack (`xoxb-` / `xoxp-` / `xoxa-` / `xoxr-` / `xapp-…`), Stripe (`sk_live_` / `sk_test_` / `rk_live_…`), OpenAI / Anthropic (`sk-…` / `sk-ant-…`), Google API (`AIza…`), Twilio (`SK…`), SendGrid (`SG.…`), npm (`npm_…`), PyPI (`pypi-…`). Treat any `<vendor-prefix>_<long-random>` the same way even if the vendor isn't listed.
- **AWS-style access keys** (`AKIA[A-Z0-9]{16}` and similar).
- **JWTs** — three base64url segments separated by dots, starting `eyJ…` (`eyJ….eyJ….<sig>`). Mask the whole token.
- **Credential-named assignments** — a value assigned to a key whose name contains `SECRET`, `KEY`, `TOKEN`, `PASSWORD` / `PASSWD` / `PWD`, or `CREDENTIAL` (`AWS_SECRET_ACCESS_KEY=…`, `API_KEY=…`, `ACCESS_TOKEN=…`, `DB_PASSWORD=…`), any casing, `=` or `:`.
- **HTTP auth headers** — `Authorization: <scheme> <value>` for **any** scheme (`Bearer …`, `Basic …`, `Token …`), plus bare `Bearer <token>` occurrences.
- **Private-key blocks** (`-----BEGIN … PRIVATE KEY-----` through its matching `END` line).
- **Credentials embedded in URLs / DSNs** (`scheme://user:password@host` — covers `https://`, `postgres://`, `redis://`, `mongodb://`, `amqp://`, …).

Replace the matched value with a fixed placeholder (`[REDACTED]`) and keep the key name / label / vendor prefix intact where one exists (`AWS_SECRET_ACCESS_KEY=[REDACTED]`, `ghp_[REDACTED]`), so a reviewer can still see *that* a credential was there without seeing *what* it was. This is a pattern pass, not a security review: it catches the common shapes above, not every secret that could exist. When a value looks credential-shaped but you're unsure (a long high-entropy string with no obvious innocent purpose), **redact anyway** — a false-positive mask costs a reviewer one clarifying glance; an unredacted real secret costs a leak. Never skip this pass because "it's just an internal issue" or "the repo is private" — it runs unconditionally before the content leaves the session, for both of the exits that produce durable artifacts (file-issue's `issue_write` body, and implement's task/ADR/child-issue drafts, any tier).

**Implement.** Map `complexity_tier` to shape per `## Tiering`. The shapes that file directly (`trivial`, `standard`) hand the plan straight to `declare_task_graph` (or `use_template`) — tasks derived from the `must_cover` EARS set, the single graph-level `exec` acceptance criterion from `## EARS & gate` attached, `coverage_target` compiled into its `check` command. `complex` does not do this directly — it enters the **reviewed decomposition sub-flow** below before anything is filed.

**Complex-tier reviewed decomposition.** A `complex`-tier plan is never dispatched as a one-shot mega-graph — the contract is explicit that this requirement gates the implement exit, not a preference to apply when convenient. Instead:

1. **Propose seam boundaries.** Split the plan along natural subsystem lines (the same lines `tier_rationale` already named as the reason for the `complex` tier) — not an arbitrary task count, but boundaries where the work genuinely decouples.
2. **Draft an ADR list**, one entry per seam decision worth recording (why this boundary, what it trades off), using the repo's existing ADR convention.
3. **Draft a child-issue set**, one child per seam, each carrying its own slice of the `must_cover` EARS set and its own `tier_rationale` for that slice.
4. **Validate child independence before proposing the set as parallel-safe**: check disjoint file sets across children — the same disjointness a parallel `standard`-shape graph relies on (`## Tiering` names that shape but doesn't spell out the method; this step is where the method lives). Two children that would touch overlapping files are not independent — merge them into one child, or make the dependency explicit and sequence them, rather than presenting them as parallel. (A dependent child still gets its own self-contained task, never an impl→impl chain against another child's branch — see `feedback_bureau_graph_design`: k8s dependents clone the base ref, not a sibling's integration branch.)
5. **Get human approval on the whole set** — seams, ADR list, child issues, independence check — before filing anything. This is the same "show the artifact before acting" floor as any other exit, applied to the decomposition itself.
6. **File only the first slice** as a `declare_task_graph` (shaped per its own sub-tier, gated per `## EARS & gate`, redacted per above). Every other child stays a **reviewable draft** — not filed, not dispatched — until the human advances it in a later turn. Auto-filing the rest as an exit side-effect is exactly the one-shot-mega-graph failure mode this sub-flow exists to prevent.
7. **Group epic↔child honestly.** Forgejo has no native parent/child primitive. Create one `mcp__forgejo__milestone_write` milestone for the whole decomposition and cross-reference siblings by `#N` issue number in each child's body (and in the epic issue, a checklist of the `#N`s) — state this mechanism plainly rather than implying a real hierarchy exists.

**File an issue.** After the redaction pass above, call `mcp__forgejo__issue_write` with a title and body built from the plan (`problem`, `proposal`, the EARS list, `tier_rationale`, `risk_flags`, the assumptions block, `nfr_register`) — the same artifact just shown for review, not a fresh summary. This exit records the plan for later triage; it does not dispatch anything.

**Discard.** The elicitation concludes without producing implementable or trackable output — the contract's third exit, not a failure state. Take it only on the user's **explicit confirmation of the literal phrase "yes, discard"** (or an unambiguous equivalent) offered in response to a direct "discard this plan?" ask; a plain "ok", "sure", or moving on to a new topic is not that confirmation, mirroring `## Plan provenance`'s "silence is not confirmation" rule. Once confirmed, exit cleanly — no artifact is filed or dispatched.
